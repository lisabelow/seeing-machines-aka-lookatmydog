Good Girl

"Mirror, mirror on the wall: Who is the best girl of them all?"
A semantic search engine of photos of my dog Alma

by Lisa Below· CompSci for Designers 2· MA Design for Digital Futures, TH Nürnberg· Summer Semester 2026

Level 1 — The Finder

Abstract

Mirror, mirror on the wall: Who is the best girl of them all? Welcome to my personal dog pic archive. My Google Colab notebook creates a pure retrieval system without captioning or text generation. Each image is embedded once using SigLIP’s image encoder while text queries are embedded with the text encoder into the same vector space. The images are then ranked by cosine similarity. The system only compares embeddings... there is no language model interpreting or describing the images.This project builds a semantic image search engine over a personal photo archive of my dog, Alma, using SigLIP (Sigmoid Loss for Language–Image Pre-training), a contrastive dual-encoder vision-language model. Every photo is embedded once into a shared image–text vector space. Then, a natural-language query is embedded into that same space, and cosine similarity alone determines which photos come back. There is no captioning, no generation, no manual tagging anywhere in the pipeline. Across 10 varied test queries, the system reliably retrieves literal, concrete scenes but degrades sharply for abstract, personal, or purely aesthetic language, and its confidence scores turn out to be far more informative when read relative to each other than as any kind of absolute probability. The most interesting finding isn't that the system works — it's how differently it fails depending on what kind of question you ask it.

Motivation & Corpus Statement

This corpus is a personal photo archive of my dog, Alma, collected over roughly three years (2023–2026): Beach trips, snow days, city walks, hikes and a lot of ordinary photography around the apartment/house. So why did I use this corpus? Short answer: Because I want to show people pictures of my dog and also I had a shit ton of Alma pictures on my phone which were finally useful. Long answer: I chose this specific collection because I know it intimately enough to immediately notice when a retrieval system gets something wrong... which is the entire point of building a system like this rather than just trusting it. The archive started with 374 exported phone images (originally over 800). Since this exceeded the allowed range of 50–150 images, the dataset had to be carefully curated. First, I manually removed obvious redundancies, such as images containing humans or text, as well as repetitive shots, reducing the corpus to 178 images. In a second step, I reviewed the remaining images and removed another 48 photos, including near-identical burst shots and overrepresented scenes, such as multiple almost identical photos of Alma on grass with a leash or on the living-room rug. The main selection criterion was diversity rather than aesthetic quality. I intentionally kept images from different years, locations, weather conditions, and activities to prevent one repetitive setting from dominating the embedding space and influencing the retrieval results. The final corpus contained 130 images. **However, I am very much aware that the corpus is still not very diverse since it's literally 130 pics of my dog Alma in different settings. Sorry.**.

The final corpus is 130 images. Because the subject is a dog rather than a person, there are no third-party consent concerns for the primary subject. A small number of images with strangers visible in public-space backgrounds were deprioritised during curation.

  • Size: 130 images (from an original archive of >800)
  • Source: My own photography of my dog Alma, 2023–2026
  • Curation: manual first pass, then systematic removal of export duplicates and redundant near-identical compositions, keeping temporal/situational spread over aesthetic "best-of" selection

Approach

SigLIP (google/siglip-base-patch16-224) is a contrastive, dual-encoder model: one encoder for images, one for text, trained with a sigmoid loss so every image–caption pair is scored independently rather than normalised across a batch (unlike CLIP's softmax loss). There is no decoder and no language generation anywhere in this system — the model can only place an image or a piece of text somewhere in a shared vector space and report distance between them. Every one of the 124 corpus images is embedded once with the image encoder, L2-normalised, and cached to Google Drive as a .npy file, so the notebook never has to recompute embeddings on a fresh runtime. At query time, the text encoder embeds the search string into the same space, and cosine similarity (a plain dot product between normalised vectors) ranks the entire corpus against it. top_k and an optional similarity threshold are the only two retrieval parameters, both exposed directly in the Gradio interface.

Results: 10 different queries

Ten queries, chosen to probe different aspects of the embedding space: literal matches, paraphrase robustness, mood, colour, near-misses, absent concepts, spatial composition, retrieval breadth, vagueness, and out-of-vocabulary personal names. Scores are cosine similarities (roughly −1 to 1, though in practice this corpus stays within about −0.05 to 0.13).

1. An easy literal match (a concrete object/scene your corpus clearly has).
2. A synonym or paraphrase of (1) — does the score hold up, or drop a lot?
3. An abstract / mood query ("a calm moment", "chaos") — embeddings often capture *some* mood signal, but weakly.
4. A colour-based query.
5. A near-miss concept that is visually similar but wrong (e.g. query for something adjacent to what's actually in an image).
6. An absent concept — something definitely **not** in your corpus. Does the similarity correctly stay low, or does it confidently return nonsense?
7. A composition/spatial query ("X on the left of Y") — per the lab notebook, expect the bag-of-concepts weakness: swapped word order often scores almost identically.
8. A query that should return many good matches (test `top_k`).
9. A deliberately ambiguous/vague query.
10. A query built from vocabulary you don't expect SigLIP to know well (a personal name, an in-joke, a very specific technical term).

Score ceilings vary a lot by query type: concrete object queries reach ~0.09–0.13, vague/mood queries only ~0.03–0.06, and the absence probe goes negative. A single fixed similarity threshold would never work equally well across query types.

Project Reflection

Running these ten queries against an archive I know by heart was a strange experience: I could predict roughly how a person would answer each question, which made SigLIP's actual answers unexpectedly diagnostic. The score magnitudes surprised me most — I expected the model to be simply "right" or "wrong," but instead it produces a wide range of confidence levels that only make sense relative to each other, never in absolute terms. Watching the composition-swap queries return almost the same photos in almost the same order, regardless of which noun came first, was the clearest demonstration all semester of what "bag of concepts" actually means in practice, rather than as a lecture slide.

Limitations

  • Compositional / relational blindness: swapping the order of two nouns in a query barely changes the results, confirming SigLIP encodes "what is present" far more reliably than "how it's arranged."
  • Loose attribute binding: colour queries return images containing the right colour somewhere in frame, not necessarily attached to the intended object.
  • No personal or proper-noun grounding: the model has no idea my dog is named Alma, and never will without additional captioning or fine-tuning (see Level 2).
  • Small-corpus effect: with only 124 images, rare one-off scenes can't be statistically distinguished from noise the way a frequently repeated scene like "beach" can.
  • No explanation or self-reported uncertainty: SigLIP can't say why it returned an image, and has no signal for its own confidence beyond the raw similarity number.
